Problem Solving

Getting through the block

There are many, many times I've ended up stuck in this sprint. I wasn't able to solve any of the exercises from Eloquent Javascript without looking at the answers. At this stage, to be honest, I don't feel like my grasp on Javascript is comprehensive enough to even be problem solving, because I don't really understand what I'm supposed to be doing in the first place. That's where I find pseudocoding really coming in handy. Often when I'm trying to lay out the steps to take in any challenge or project in life, my first step is to write it all down with some sort of basic structure - like journalling, it helps me to be able to focus on the bigger picture all at once.

I'm definitely not feeling confident, and I think I've told myself this story that I'm imcompetent because I'm struggling so much with even the simplest exercises, and there have definitely been moments of deep frustration and complete helplessness. In saying all of this, however, by the end of the last exercise I felt like I was in a better state of flow, and felt like I understood what I was supposed to be creating, and the sense of delight and satisfaction that comes from getting something to work without looking at the answers is worth all the grind!

There's a lot of learning invlved in this process. Technically, I'm understanding the structure of javascript a lot better, and how all the different elements fit together and what each of them does. But more importantly I'm learning patience. Patience to not base my success in getting it perfect the first time, and patience in the fact that actually, it's not that important that I DO understand it all now, and trusting that it's all going to come together at the right time later down the track.

What are my strategies like?

  • Pseudocode
    Feels great. Its suits my style of thinking and learning, and gives me a record of how I was thinking prior to solving the problem, which helps me to see how I need to improve my approaches.
  • Trying something
    Yup this is good too, often my first step actually, just blindly throwing some code at the console haha. It's easy to do and gives me at least something to work with, instead of staring at a blank screen.
  • Rubber Ducky method
    Haven't actually tried this one yet. I know I should because it will help me clarify what I need to be doing, and highlight the parts I understand well and don't understand well. But hasn't happened yet
  • Reading error messages
    Haven't tried this one either. Or maybe I have. I don't think I found it very helpful because I often didn't understand what it was referring to!
  • Console.logging
    Haven't reeeeally tried this one either. It took me a while to get used to dev tools (but once I did they were great!!), and I suspect it will be the same for console logging. I know it's a great tool but I don't feel comfortable using it yet.
  • Googling
    Yep, real easy to go to. However I don't understand much of what the responses are in the online forums the queries are often posted on, so I've noticed myself relying on it less and less.
  • Asking peers
    Yeah I like doing this. It feels best to get a response from someone that has likely been through a very similar learning curve recently, as I feel like they are in the best position to understand what I am stuck on and what I need to know, and be able to communicate it with good speed and language.
  • Asking coaches
    I haven't really felt like I've got to a stage where I've exhausted my options enough to go to the coaches for help which is great, but it's nice to know that I have a safety net I can really rely on if I really really need it!
  • Improving process with reflection
    Yeah I very instinctively see the value in this, and if I've just finished learning an entire process or exercise, I'll often try and write the whole thing down from my head just to make sure it's settled in my brain properly.
