Making Mindfulness Millenial-Friendly

Checking In on Mindfulness Practices

So I actually already do a daily practice of answering a series of questions I have set myself for personal reflection and habit building. It's amazing! You should all do it! And actually the first of my questions is "Did you spend 15 minutes of peaceful silent time with yourself?" Doing this reflective practice with a other person is doubly effective - there is something about that external accountability that I personally find more motivating.

Having someone you can check in with weekly or daily is beneficial for any habit-building, as it gives you the space to be able to reflect on what you feel like you're doing well and not doing well, and whether it's helpful and purposeful or not.

Keeping the Momentum Going

Out of the three suggested techniques for maintaning my mindfulness practice, I guess I am already doing the first one ("Have a buddy"). I find talking about it often keeps it on top of my mind, and easier to put into practice during everyday life. I also like the idea of "One breath a day", as it relates well to the BJ Fogg method we have already looked at, through minimising the task to the extent that it is ultra-achieveable!

Aside from that, I actually really enjoy practising mindfulness when I am gardening, which I do a couple times weekly at the moment. I find it really good to link the habit to a task I already do (just like Fogg says!).

A Very Important P.S.

The most fascinating and beneficial thing I am getting from reading Search Inside Yourself is most definitely the way it disarms failure. Failure is a concept that creates massive mental and emotional blocks for many people - myself absolutely included - and this book does a really good job of looking at what we assume failure and success are, and actually taking the time to redefine them appropriately. I have spent a good amount of my life basing my "success" value on my ability to get things right the first time, or in a short amount of time. This book has taught me that, actually no, success is based on accepting the first-time failure as a very normal and natural thing, and focussing on how you respond to that. In mindfulness, that translates to bringing my attention back to my breath when it wanders. In coding, that translates to looking at the dishevelled broken code I have just written, and understanding how to fix it. This has been HUGE! It has the power to completely transform my self-image and self-worth, as well as transforming the way I choose to engage with challenges and setbacks. I could go on and on about this, because it really is so revelatory for me. So yes! Thank you Chade-Meng Tan!
